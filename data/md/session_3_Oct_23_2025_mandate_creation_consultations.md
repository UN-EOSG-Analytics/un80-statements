**IAHWG | Carolyn Schwalger | Co-Chair**

Good morning everybody.
Now, as we turn to the questions posed to the briefers at the Mandate creation briefing on 13th October, we wish to respond to the question posed to us as Co Chairs about how lessons learned from the last mandate review and other processes have been reflected in our consideration.
As Co Chairs, we have spoken with several individuals from Member States and the Secretariat who were involved in the last mandate review.
We discovered three key learnings.
Firstly, the previous exercise on review of existing individual mandates was a complex and time consuming process.
Ultimately, the value of the technical exercise was undermined by the prevailing political realities.
Secondly, it was apparent that the tools necessary to support a deeper understanding of the overall mandates for framework, such as a mandate registry, were unavailable, underdeveloped or fledgling at best.
Thirdly, we learned about the importance of improving understanding between mandate creation and the budgetary process.
We have attempted to build these learnings into the approach we are taking in this Working Group.
Further, in response to another question, we can confirm that we have also begun engaging with Member States who have recently led relevant intergovernmental processes to see what lessons or ideas we can carry over into this Working Group.
One example of this is the General assembly revitalization process.
We are also engaging with the Chairs of the Main Committees to understand how the issue of mandate creation, implementation and review is being considered in their Committees and what this could mean for the Working Group's own deliberations.

We already separate preambular and operative paragraphs and identify the actions, entities and deliverables in each resolution.
We were able to do this otherwise very painstaking task by AI, which scans and classifies keywords across thousands of documents, and I will get into that shortly afterwards and how we use the AI.

The main constant will be skills, as I just mentioned, not cost.
Most of the skills we need are found among young people, and that is why your support for UN rejuvenation is so critical.
Finally, on scope, the mandates.un.org portal covers identify mandates coming from bodies serviced by the UN Secretariat.
In other words, bodies such as the General Assembly, Security Council, ecosoc and so on.
Specialized agencies are invited to develop their own registries for their mandating bodies, but using the shared data standards so that information can connect across.
So let me now address a group of questions related to resourcing of registries and mandates more generally.
So this was the second bigger group of questions and first on the registry development.
The current version of mandates was built by a very small staff, small team in our office using skills we are promoting through the UN 2.0 initiative, I.e. with new capabilities in data design and digital delivery.
Second, on the financial impact of mandates themselves.
Each mandate, even a simple one, carries cost for creation, implementation and review at a minimum.
That could mean staff time, translation, editing, or meetings.
For such routine tasks, there are standard cost estimates that are transparently laid out in the memorandum, that is before the General Committee at the beginning of each session of the General Assembly.
We refer to this document in our answers about $11,000 per day for meetings of up to two days and $25,000 for editing and translation, not the initial drafting of a report of around 8,500 words.
Third, on what some of you call the cost of ambiguity.
We don't really have a systematic data on the financial impact of vaguely vague language and resolutions, but we know that it matters.
When the intent of a mandate is not clear, then we need extra consultations, coordination, which takes both time and resources.
And that is why clarity on intent really matters.
Finally, there are questions on how time intensive certain groups of mandates are in general act.
As the number and complexity of mandates in a pillar grows, so do resource demands on Member states, more meetings, reports and negotiations, and on UN entities that must serve them.
We can show at the registry briefing next week how to find large groups of mandates, if you like.
The simple takeaway here is that resources are not only financial, there is also human and temporal the time, attention and clarity needed to make each mandate deliverable.
The next large group of questions was about artificial intelligence and digital tools and how they're being used to support mandate work.
First, on what AI does.
It can augment research capacity.
As I just explained, we use that to build up registry of mandates, for example, to help identify keywords, recurring phrases, overlaps and similarities across a large body of mandate text.
It is an analytical support tool, not one to write or negotiate resolutions.
That should of course remain a human process.
Second, on data for mandates.un.org the system processes only publicly available documents, resolutions, decisions and budgets, all sourced from the UN Digital library.
Third, on data security and quality.
This site only uses open sources.
No internal, confidential or personal data have been used.
AI helps process large amounts of data quickly with built in checks and human reviews for quality.
Some variation is normal and our team is improving the system over time.
Finally, on future digital tools, we are continuing to build on your feedback over time.
New digital tools, including AI capabilities, may allow for quicker comparison of text, of visual or of visual mapping of how mandates relate to each other.
The goal is to make information easier to find and to understand.
The simple takeaway is that AI can support analysis, not make final decisions.
It can help us save time and improve visibility while we keep human judgment at the center of the process.
Finally, let me speak to questions on several broader issues that were made from review clauses and flexibility in tasking to guidance for Member States and the timeline for next steps.
First, on review clauses, most mandates still lack explicit review or end mechanisms.
Over 80% of mandates cited in the current program budget do not specify how or when they should be reviewed.
Changing that practice would make mandates easier to assess and adapt over time.
We understand that the Co Chairs will hold another briefing on mandate review later this year.
Second, on the Secretary General's flexibility, the report recommends allowing the Secretary General to assign tasks based on comparative advantage, meaning the entity best placed to deliver, rather than prescribing one entity in the resolution text.
And in fact, that has been the norm over the years and it's only in recent years that it has become more prescriptive.
So what that means is that referring only to the Secretary General, that the United nations or UN system would be the preferred or the easiest way to do assignment based on comparative advantage.
Third, to better support Member States, the Secretary General proposed three main actions.
Improved human support for delegates during the drafting, Enhanced Inter Secretariat cooperation to support visibility across intergovernmental bodies and digital tools.
Fourth, on how to ensure new mandates are backed by resources or accompanied by a review of existing ones.
Our suggestion here included using review clauses, consolidating agenda items, or proposing the discontinuation of outdated outputs, which is an authority that the Secretary General intends to make make purposeful use of.
Finally, on the timeline for Secretary General General related actions, many of these actions are already in progress, including the continued improvements of mandates.un.org Fuller implementation is targeted for 2026 in line with the available capacity and resources.
We will keep you updated both in a General assembly plenary and in this Working Group.
The key message here is that we want to support your work on impactful mandate design, efficient delivery and accountable reviews, all grounded in evidence and dialogue.

**EU | Representative | (EU)**

Distinguished Co. Chairs, Distinguished colleagues, I have the honor to deliver this statement on behalf of the European Union and its member states.
The candidate countries, Turkey and North Macedonia, Montenegro, Ukraine, the Republic of Moldova, Bosnia and Herzegovina and Georgia, as well as Armenia, Andorra, Monaco and San Marino, align themselves with the Statement on Mandate Creation, a topic of today.
We must acknowledge that effective mandates demand effective design and have full visibility across the entire mandate system, which includes what mandates already exist on the same or related topics, who and how is implementing them and with what results.
Therefore, we welcome measures designed to first amplify the overall impact and sustainability of new mandates and eliminate implementation ambiguities.
This should be done through the adoption of concise action oriented and outcome driven language, the implementation and impact of mandates should be measurable and system wide evaluation will be critical.
Second, introduce sunset clauses with a robust mechanism for periodic review to minimize perpetual extensions and ensure mandates evolve or conclude in alignment with their objectives, as well as review clauses to ensure that regular assessment and evaluation of the relevance and efficiency of the mandates can take place.
