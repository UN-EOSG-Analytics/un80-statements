**Costa Rica | Representative**

Thank you very much.
Dear co facilitators, Co chairs, I'm sorry.
It's great to see you coordinating our works and congratulations from my delegation for all the work you have done so far.
Excellencies, Dear distinguished colleagues, we are operating in a context of growing financial pressures that affect the predictability required for effective mandate delivery.
This reality underscores the need to strengthen responsible financing, governance and administration so that the United nations can continue to fulfill the mandates entrusted to it.
Mandate implementation is where credibility is tested.
Allow me to highlight three points and four possible step forwards.
First, the reporting burden continues to expand, largely because we Member States continue to request new reports.
Costa Rica supports a careful review of periodicities, duplications and mandates whose reporting requirements may be no longer necessary.
The goal is to improve the quality, usefulness and timeliness of reporting.
While each committee has its own dynamics, more deliberate planning and sequencing of meeting time between bureaus and the Secretariat can support more effective workload management, acknowledging that only part of the cost structure is truly adjustable.
Second, a more coherent United nations requires stronger system wide coordination.
Clarifying the division of labor on the basis of comparative advantage can reduce overlap and increase impact.
For Costa Rica, this must be guided by transparent and more inclusive intergovernmental oversight across the UN system as a whole, particularly in the funds and programs and within the governance of their executive boards.
Coordination efforts should respect established governance arrangements while also identifying areas where those arrangements could be refined to support more coherent planning and delivery.
Third, mandate implementation, budgeting and liquidity are inseparable.
Persistent cash constraints require responsible forms of flexibility within existing governance frameworks.
Better internal reprogramming, a better a more strategic use of existing reserve and more coherent resource planning across related clusters can help sustain operations and reduce fragmentation.
Pool or joint mechanisms may also help reduce structural competition for resources.
Looking ahead, Costa Rica supports first, a focused mandate implementation dialogue, a more comprehensive one and a continuous one.
Second, evidence based reviews of reporting cycles.
Third, assessments of the cost implications of different reporting and meeting models, modalities and fourth, an annual exchange with the Secretariat to better align mandates, capacities and resources.
Costa Rica remains committed to a United nations that is coherent, efficient and capable of delivering the results that the international community expects.
I thank you.

**Indonesia | Representative**

Thank you. Co Chairs Indonesia extends appreciation to you for the inclusive, transparent and constructive engagement in the continuation of this process.
We also thank all briefers for the responses and also for the presentation last week.
Today's discussion on mandate implementation is among the utmost importance of the overall process.
This is the point where decisions taken are translated into real change on the ground.
It is where expectations must turn into action and where the true effectiveness of the United nations is ultimately measured.
Allow us to share our two key reflections on today's topic.
First, on the design of the reports.
From the last briefing, we noted a persistent pattern that a significant portion of reports are recurring, yet those do not always yield new or meaningful values.
Indonesia believes that future reports must shift from repetition to relevance and offering deeper insights in a more comprehensive and measurable way.
In this regard, we are of the view that areas of improvement could be applied to the report designs, such as maintaining a similar structure of reports on targeted focus with updated contents in its issuance of reports, that this will enable us to track progress and identify gaps.
Meanwhile, emerging issues could be highlighted in a separate chapter with brief details.
Second, on the issue of overlapping mandate citations, while entities might cite similar mandates, a similar citation does not necessarily mean similar implementation in practice.
Entities may apply the same mandate to different regions, constituencies or operational contexts.
We therefore need a mechanism that traces the end use of these mandates, clarifying what activities are undertaken, where they occur and with what concrete impacts.
The function parameters presented in the response to the last briefing, ranging from normative support and policy advice to data capacity building and service delivery, could be a starting point as practical tools to map complementarities and reduce unnecessary duplication.
Co Chairs to conclude, rest assured that Indonesia stands ready to engage constructively to ensure mandate implementation remains purposeful, coherent and impactful.
Thank you.

**Algeria | Representative**

Thank you for giving me the floor.
Algeria aligns itself with the statement delivered by Iraq on behalf of G77 and China and welcomes the opportunity to engage constructively in the ongoing discussions on unity.
We share the collective aspiration for a United nations that is more coherent, more coordinated and more responsive.
In this regard, we have taken note of proposals that could allow the Secretariat to set or adjust priorities on the basis of internal consideration.
We wish to reiterate that the strategic direction of the Organization is determined exclusively by Member States.
Mandates, whether political, peacekeeping, humanitarian or development related, cannot be recalibrated due to financial constraints or operational pressures.
Their implementation is not discretionary, nor can it be delayed or reshaped outside of intergovernmental decisions.
We also recognize the value of enhancing efficiency and fostering innovation in reporting and resolution making.
However, these efforts must not weaken Member States ability to exercise their work.
What is required is improved analytical depth, clearer methodologies for the preparation of reports, more rigorous self evaluation by the Secretariat and increased transparency.
We need to make the best of the accumulated institutional knowledge that the organization has gathered through its 80 years of existence.
Furthermore, reforms that affect Africa must be undertaken in close consultation with African states, the African Union, and regional organizations that hold direct responsibilities in peace, security and development on the continent.
Inclusion and partnership are indispensable for a credible and sustainable reform process.
In this regard, we caution against any measures that could negatively impact peace, security and development in Africa, and recall that the United nations bears a historical responsibility toward the continent, one that this reform process must uphold and strengthen, not depart from.
Finally, we underscore the importance of Excuse me, we underscore the importance of maintaining coherence across all UNH work streams.
Decisions taken in one area, whether structural, operational or budgetary, inevitably affect mandate implementation in others.
To avoid fragmented or unintended outcomes, the process must be considered and managed holistically, with a full appreciation of its interdependences.
Thank you.

**Kyrgyzstan | Representative**

Thank you. Co Chairs Excellencies.
Let me begin by thanking the Co Chairs for convening this important meeting and for their continued efforts to advance the reform of the United Nations.
Today, the United nations stand at a decisive moment.
The UNAT reform must be more than an administrative exercise.
It must help the organization act faster, work smarter and deliver better Results.
With the UN system resources expected to decrease by almost 1/4 by 2026, we cannot afford excessive bureaucracy.
We must focus on what truly effective, coordinated and accountable implementation of mandates.
Kyrgyzstan believes that the success of reform depends on our ability to to strengthen cooperation among UN aid entities.
We need to reduce duplication, improve coordination, and ensure that every mandate has clear objectives and measurable impact.
The UN should move from fragmented and parallel efforts toward a more integrated and complementary approach, especially across the peace, development and humanitarian pillars.

Efficiency means not doing more with less, but doing better with what we have in a focused and measurable way.

The real measure of UN success lies in its ability to prevent conflict, build peace and include people.
In these efforts, we emphasize preventive diplomacy, regional cooperation and inclusive peace building that fully engage women and youth as agents of stability and resilience.
Co Chairs we stand at a historic crossroads.
The choice is ours to remain constrained by outdated procedures or to rise as a force for decisive and principled actions.
Kyrgyzstan calls and all Member States to show political will, vision and courage so that the unity reform becomes a true turnpoint leading to an organization that acts swiftly, clearly and in service of all people.
